Sun 11-Mar-2001

No escape
'Suburban flight' doesn't free people from city-style congestion
By Ben Jones
Post-Crescent staff writer

New houses sprout up in the Country Meadows development north of Everglade Road in Greenville, a town that grew 80 percent in the 1990s. A survey indicated that a quarter of the residents moved there to escape congestion. Post-Crescent photo by Sharon Cekada
In the 1960s the exodus began in places such as Detroit, Chicago and Milwaukee.

Residents abandoned urban centers for the wide streets and cul-de-sacs of the suburbs.

It's called suburban flight, and local planners say it has arrived in the Fox Cities.

"What we are seeing here is a re-creation of the 1960s," said Dave Muench, a community development specialist with the Outagamie County University of Wisconsin-Extension office. "We're experiencing an urban exodus on a smaller scale."

The Fox Cities, unlike larger cities such as Detroit and Milwaukee, are still growing.

In the 1990s, Outagamie County villages grew by 12.5 percent and cities grew by 5.3 percent. But U.S. Census numbers released Thursday indicate the Valley's population growth is increasingly taking place in the towns.

In Outagamie County, towns in the 1990s grew by nearly 30 percent, their fastest growth rate in at least 50 years.

"People want safety, they want lower taxes, they want bigger lots and they want new homes," Muench said.

The UW-Extension has an ongoing research project into rapidly growing Fox Valley communities.

A community study included surveys of residents in Greenville, a town that grew 80 percent in the 1990s, Harrison, a town that grew by 80 percent, and Buchanan, a town that grew by 135 percent.

Muench said the surveys indicate most people who moved into the towns came from a city within 10 miles. About a third of the residents of Darboy, an unincorporated community that takes in parts of the towns of Buchanan and Harrison, moved there from Appleton.

The 466 surveys returned from Darboy indicated that the most common reason residents moved to Darboy was to escape congestion.

In Greenville, a survey returned by more than 580 people indicated a quarter of residents moved there to escape congestion.

But Muench said problems people try to escape often follow them to rural areas.

"They moved out there to get away from people and away from congestion, but now they have people and they have congestion," Muench said.

On the comment portion of the Darboy Community Survey, people responded with regrets such as "Traffic has increased severely and no relief planned" and development is "out of control, growing too fast."

Betty Nordeng, a planner with the East Central Wisconsin Regional Planning Commission, said growth can get ahead of communities.

"It takes a while to catch up, especially with road networks," she said. "When you are growing rapidly, sometimes you create narrow corridors where you need to expand but you can't quite fit the road."

She said this has created some challenges in the reconstruction of County KK, which forms the border between Outagamie and Calumet counties.

She said one intersection is placed too close to State 441, creating traffic backups.

In Grand Chute, Community Development Director Allen Davis said the town has taken measures to address its rapid growth.

During the 1990s, when the town grew 27 percent to 18,392, the town expanded its board from three to five members, added administrative staff and adopted zoning ordinances.

Davis said town officials continually re-evaluate what they are doing to address traffic, environment and infrastructure concerns.

"I think the town views good growth as a good thing," he said. "If it's the right type of land use, it's positive for the town as well as the whole Valley."

Communities have strong incentive to grow.

Swelling census numbers are important for municipalities.

The main purpose of the numbers being released on all 50 states this month is to draw legislative district lines, but the census has importance for many government functions.

Wisconsin cities, villages and towns state funds based on population as reported in the census, and updated through annual estimates from the Demographic Services Center within the state Department of Administration.

Don Harrier, chief of the center, listed federal benefits including Medicaid, highway planning and construction grants, foster care funding and special education money as being dependent on the census.

State law even limits how many liquor licenses a municipality can issue based on the population count.

Muench said state and federal grants make it easier for communities to build roads and schools, which in turn cause more people to build homes in a community.

Mike Hendrick, a planner with the Outagamie County Planning Office, said rapid development is nothing new to the Fox Valley.

"We've seen surges in the past." he said.

Outagamie County's overall growth rate of 14.6 percent in the 1990s was exceeded by its 17 percent rate in the 1960s and 25 percent in the 1950s.

Hendrick said that in the 1950s, people were moving to urban areas such as Appleton. Fringe development began in the 1960s.

"First everyone moved into the cities; then everyone began moving out," he said.

Outagamie County Planning Director Bob Stadel said people's desire for large lots, combined with their willingness to commute long distances, has contributed to development in the towns.

He said this type of development can lead to pollution and increased utility costs, but he doesn't expect this trend to change any time soon.

"It's just a fact," he said. "We aren't ready yet, in my opinion, to accept that we can live and work in literally the same neighborhoods."

Muench said rapid population growth in the Fox Valley will likely continue. He said it is important for municipalities to find ways to work together on issues that, in the end, affect everyone.

"It's going to take a concerted effort to get this under control," he said.

* Post-Crescent City Editor David Horst contributed to this story.
